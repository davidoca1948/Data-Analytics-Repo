My Script for Data Presentation

Slide 1: Title Slide
“Iowa Liquor Sales Breakdown: Gin Insights (2014)”

“Today I’ll be walking you through an overview of how Gin performed across Iowa in 2014.
The goal of this analysis is to understand where Gin is succeeding, where it’s struggling, and where there are clear opportunities or risks moving forward.
This is meant to support smarter decisions around distribution, marketing, and growth strategy.”

Slide 2: Scenario – Let’s Understand Where We Are

“Before we talk about results, I want to level‑set on what we’re trying to understand.
This analysis looks at Gin sales throughout the year across counties and stores in Iowa. We’re asking a few key business questions:
Where is Gin performing well?
Where is it underperforming?
And how does Gin compare to competing categories like Vodka?

Slide 3: Summary of Stats (2014)
“At a high level, Gin generated approximately $10.7 million in revenue across Iowa in 2014.
The average bottle price was about $12.67, which tells us Gin sits comfortably in the mid‑price range — accessible, but not positioned as a premium-only product.
Polk County stands out as the top-performing county, which signals strong urban demand.
This overview gives us the baseline — Gin is a meaningful revenue driver, but performance varies widely by location.”

Slide 4: Strengths – Best Performing Counties

“Looking at strengths, we see that Gin performs exceptionally well in counties like Polk, Johnson, and Linn.
Polk County alone generates significantly more revenue than other counties, indicating strong consumer demand in high‑population or urbanized areas.
This tells us two things:
First, Gin clearly resonates in certain markets.
Second, when distribution and demand align, Gin can scale very effectively.”

Slide 5: Weaknesses – Worst Performing Counties

“On the other end of the spectrum, we see several counties generating very low Gin revenue.
Counties like Adams, Taylor, and Davis show minimal sales activity, which may point to limited distribution, lower demand, or lack of awareness.
This isn’t necessarily a failure, but it does highlight uneven market penetration.
If left unaddressed, these gaps can limit overall growth.”

If we look at one example where they have been many sales despite not having much population. We can see Dickinson county has sky rocketed in sales despite only having 
around 16,000 population where other low performing only have 3-4,000 less population but way worse sales. This shows when we can focus a little more on these low populated areas 
we can still make big impact and get the best out of them.

Slide 6: Opportunities – Population vs Sales

“One of the clearest opportunities comes from looking at sales alongside population.
Higher‑population counties consistently generate higher Gin revenue, which suggests demand scales with population density.
This creates an opportunity to focus marketing efforts, promotions, or expanded distribution in populated regions where Gin already has momentum — rather than spreading resources evenly across the state.”

Slide 7: Threats – Gin vs Vodka

“No analysis is complete without addressing competition.
When we compare Gin to Vodka, we see that Vodka dominates total sales, accounting for roughly 90% of transactions, while Gin represents a much smaller share.
Customers may choose Vodka over Gin based on price, availability, or cocktail trends.
This presents a real threat: if consumer preferences shift further toward Vodka, Gin could lose market share unless it differentiates itself.”

Slide 8: Recommendations for the Future

“Based on this analysis, there are three clear actions to consider.
First, expand distribution in underperforming counties where there may be untapped potential.
Second, double down on promoting bestselling Gin products in high‑performing counties.
And third, focus growth strategies on high‑population areas where demand is already proven.
Together, these steps help strengthen Gin’s position while mitigating competitive pressure from Vodka.”
